Detailed Market Assessment

ICT in Iraq isn’t just about fibre cables and towers any more; it’s a mosaic of integrators, cloud vendors, SaaS start‑ups and thousands of resellers stitching together connectivity and applications for a market hungry for digital services. Below is a structured assessment of that landscape.

Market Overview – Number of Players

  • System integrators and ICT service providers: Iraq’s enterprise IT market is still dominated by a handful of large integrators—Onyxes Technologies in Baghdad (over 160 employees and four operations centres) describes itself as a leading systems integrator and technology service provider. Regional firms such as METCO (Kuwait’s MassilaTech) also operate offices in Iraq, employing more than 600 professionals across the Gulf and offering unified communications, networks and data‑centre solutions. Alongside these, several dozen local integrators (e.g., Selina‑ICT in Erbil, Sharpsoft, Snawbar, Freeone Space, Ankido, SharpSoft, etc.) provide data‑centre, virtualization, security and managed‑services capabilities. Combining local integrators and regional entrants yields ~50–60 active system integrators/ICT service providers.
  • Hosting and cloud services: TechBehemoths’ July 2025 listing shows 14 companies in Iraq providing hosting and cloud services. These include Hoster.iq, which registers Iraqi domains and offers high‑performance hosting with branches in Baghdad, Basra and Erbil, and IQ Hosting, which has offered shared/reseller hosting, VPS and dedicated servers since 2008.
  • SaaS start-ups: According to Tracxn, the SaaS sector in Iraq comprised 26 companies as of 12 July 2025, of which one had external funding. Notable examples include Rigt (a fintech SaaS provider) and Lezzoo (an on‑demand superapp) with combined revenues around US$26.9 million.
  • Resellers and small ISPs: Beyond licensed providers, Iraq has a vast informal ecosystem of Wi‑Fi resellers—estimated at 8,000 in our prior fixed‑line report—who resell bandwidth from national carriers and international ISPs. Many of these resellers also offer basic hosting, cloud storage and VoIP services.

Actionable Insights for Market Entry

  • Partner with established integrators: Local system integrators (Onyxes, Ankido, Selina‑ICT) have deep relationships with government and oil‑sector clients. Collaborating with them can accelerate market entry and mitigate regulatory hurdles.
  • Target Erbil for SaaS and cloud: The Kurdistan Region’s relative stability and concentration of tech start‑ups make Erbil a logical base for SaaS and cloud services. Opening a small development centre or partnering with local incubators can build brand presence.
  • Offer Arabic‑first UCaaS/CCaaS solutions: There are few local UCaaS and CCaaS providers (≈10). A platform with Arabic‑language interfaces and integration with Iraqi CRM/ERP systems would stand out.
  • Comply with CMC licensing: Before offering internet or cloud services, secure the appropriate CMC licence. Factor in a three‑year licence term and allow for MoC approvals for infrastructure deployment.
  • Invest in security and data‑centre services: Cybersecurity demand is rising, and government agencies are building data‑centre capacity. Managed security services or SOC offerings tied to data‑centre colocation could differentiate a new entrant.

Push-to-Talk (PTT) Market Analysis

A deep dive into the Push-to-Talk over Cellular (PoC) market in Iraq, a critical communication technology for business and mission-critical operations.

PMR vs. PoC Technology

Before PoC became a mature technology, Professional Mobile Radio (PMR) was the standard for mission-critical users. Understanding the shift from PMR to PoC is key to grasping the market opportunity. PoC leverages cellular networks to offer enhanced features and global coverage, dramatically lowering the entry barrier for many businesses.

Feature Traditional PMR (Analogue, DMR, TETRA) PoC (Push-to-Talk over Cellular)
TechnologyRadio-based (UHF/VHF)Cellular (LTE/5G) & Wi-Fi
CoverageLimited, requires repeatersNationwide/Global
Data CapabilitiesLimited (Text, GPS)Rich (Video, Multimedia)
Infrastructure CostHigh (Dedicated infrastructure)Low (Uses existing cell networks)
Cost ModelHigh CAPEX, lower OPEXLow CAPEX, subscription OPEX

Potential PoC Market Size in Iraq by Industry

The total addressable market for PoC devices in Iraq is estimated to be over 32,500 units, with significant opportunities across various sectors. This analysis highlights key industries and their potential demand.

Industry Deep Dive & Use Cases

Private Security (~5,000 units)

This is potentially the largest market. PoC solves major pain points of traditional radios like limited coverage and high cost, while adding features like GPS tracking and video, enhancing safety and efficiency for security firms.

Energy & Utilities (~16,500 units)

Includes Oil & Gas, Electricity, and Water. While some sites have existing private networks, PoC is ideal for coordinating field maintenance and managing distributed assets over wide areas. ATEX (explosion-proof) devices are critical for the Oil & Gas sector.

Transportation (~3,000 units)

Airports and railways require seamless communication for ground handling, security, and operational coordination. PoC provides the wide-area coverage needed for these large, complex environments.

Healthcare & Hospitality (~7,000 units)

Hospitals and large hotels can use PoC for coordinating staff, managing security, and improving service efficiency without the need for expensive private radio infrastructure.

Conclusion & Go-to-Market

There is a significant, untapped market for PoC solutions in Iraq. Success depends on educating end-users on the benefits over traditional radio, targeting key industries like private security and utilities, and leveraging the nationwide coverage of cellular networks. Telcos like Asiacell and Zain are well-positioned to capture this market by offering PoC as a value-added service to their existing and new enterprise customers.

Key Growth Trends

Several powerful trends are converging to accelerate Iraq's digital journey, creating a cycle of investment, innovation, and adoption.

1. Digital Transformation

Government initiatives to digitize public services are creating massive demand for ICT solutions, modernizing administration and citizen services.

2. Cloud Adoption

Businesses are increasingly moving to the cloud for scalability and efficiency, driving strong demand for IaaS, PaaS, and SaaS solutions across all sectors.

3. Infrastructure Investment

Major investments in fiber optics and data centers, like the IRAQ-IXP, are enhancing connectivity and laying the groundwork for a robust digital economy.

4. International Partnerships

Local firms are partnering with global tech leaders to introduce cutting-edge solutions, bridging the technology gap and accelerating market maturity.
